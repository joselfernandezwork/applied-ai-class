Neural Networks: The Basics

A network of mathematical functions that transform inputs into outputs

🧠 Human analogy
Think of a neural network like a team of specialists in an assembly line. Each "neuron" receives a signal, decides how important it is, and passes it forward — weighted by how much it should matter. The team was trained on billions of examples until it got very good at its job.

Each arrow carries a learned weight. The network was trained to adjust these weights until outputs improved. Modern frontier models have 1–3+ trillion of them.

02
The Transformer: The Key Innovation (2017)

Inside one "Transformer Block" — repeated 80–120 times in a modern LLM

🔍 Multi-Head Self-Attention
Each token looks at every other token and decides how much to "attend" to it. When processing "bank," it figures out from context: river bank or financial institution? This happens in parallel across 12–96 attention heads simultaneously.
🔢 Feed-Forward Network
After attention, two linear transformations with a non-linear activation. This is where factual knowledge is stored — facts learned during training live here as learned weight patterns.
⚖️ Layer Normalization
Rescales activations to keep numbers stable during training. Without this, signals either explode to infinity or vanish to zero in very deep networks. Critical for models with 100+ layers.
➕ Residual Connections
The original input is added back after each sub-layer (x = x + attention(x)). This "skip connection" lets gradients flow backward during training without getting lost. It's why deep models actually learn.

How "bank" knows it means a riverbank — not a financial institution

👁️ Self-attention in action: When processing "bank", each of the 12–96 attention heads computes a relevance score against every other token. Because "river" scores highest, the model embeds a geographic meaning — not a financial one. This whole process runs in parallel for every token at once, which is why Transformers are so fast.

⚡ Bigger ≠ Always Better: Smaller, well-tuned models (like Claude Haiku 4.5 or GPT-5.4 mini) often outperform larger ones on specific tasks — and cost 10–100× less to run. Modern AI is about the right model for the right job.
04
What Makes LLMs Different from Earlier AI

The shift from narrow AI to general-purpose language models

📚
Pre-trained on Everything
Trained on hundreds of billions of words from books, websites, code, and scientific papers. They "know" most of what humans have written.
🎯
Fine-tuned on Behavior
After pre-training, human raters give feedback on outputs. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) shapes the model to be helpful, harmless, and honest.
🔮
Emergent Abilities
At sufficient scale, LLMs develop abilities they weren't explicitly trained for: reasoning, analogical thinking, code generation, and multi-step planning.

🎯 Key Takeaway: An LLM is a very deep neural network (Transformer architecture) trained to predict the next token. At scale, this simple objective produces something that can write, reason, code, analyze, and create. You don't need to understand the math — but knowing the architecture helps you use the tools better.
